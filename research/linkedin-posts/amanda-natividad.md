Search isn't dead.

It's so alive that it now happens across Google, Amazon, YouTube, Reddit, AI tools, and all kinds of other places people go to look for answers.

Search is a behavior.

A few recent receipts:

- Rand Fishkin's research shows Google is still absurdly massive. In his analysis of the top 5,000 most-visited websites, Google was as large as the next 13 sites combined. It explains why search so often gets credit for demand that was created somewhere else first.
- Cyrus Shepard argues the job is not just to rank, or even just to win the click. It's to look like the result most likely to satisfy the searcher — and then actually do it.
- Ross Simmonds found that Reddit is showing up earlier in the buyer journey than many SaaS brands probably realize. In his research, buyers were often seeing Reddit discussions before vendor websites, even on general category searches.
- Wil Reynolds learned the hard way that if your strongest proof points aren't published, AI and search systems may piece together weaker third-party evidence to fill the gap.

If you want to try Zero Click Marketing for yourself, don't start with a campaign. Start with a constraint.

One thing B2B growth marketer Brendan Hufford did early on was a "100 Days of SEO" project. Every day, he published a YouTube video, podcast episode, and blog post about SEO. Useful ideas, shared in public.

This is what Zero Click Marketing ultimately is. It's not one big swing — it's a body of work.

If you want to try this yourself, you don't need 100 days. But you do need structure. Here's how I'd approach it:

1/ Pick a lane. Be specific. "Marketing" isn't enough. Is it... growth marketing? Ok. B2B or B2C? What sector? At what level?

2/ Define your differentiator. Why you? What perspective are you bringing?

3/ Choose your audience. Write for someone you can mentor, someone you admire, and someone who's your peer.

4/ Set a cadence. 2–3x/week is enough. Daily if you're ambitious.

5/ Keep it standalone. Every post should be useful on its own without requiring a click.

And most importantly: Don't measure this like a campaign. You're not looking for immediate conversions. You're looking for signals that your audience is paying attention.

Because by the time someone clicks, they've already made their decision.

**Content:**

Every marketer wants to know: what's my audience actually typing into ChatGPT?

We shipped a feature in SparkToro that answers this.

I ran a report on "forward-thinking B2B content marketing professionals" and here's what jumped out:

This audience uses AI tools A LOT MORE than the average web user. +137%, +125%, even +416% above average on some tools.

Top AI prompt topics, ranked by affinity:
- Content Marketing Strategies — 88/100
- SEO Best Practices — 86
- Lead Generation Techniques — 83
- Social Media Advertising — 75
- Email Marketing Performance — 72

Their Google searches are tactical and specific. They know what they need and they're looking for it. Their AI prompts are strategic and broad — they're trying to figure out what to do next.

This can hugely impact your content strategy:
→ SEO content should stay specific and how-to oriented
→ Content optimized for AI answers should be more strategic, frameworks-oriented, and opinionated

The differences between how people search and how people prompt is one of the most interesting things happening in marketing right now.
